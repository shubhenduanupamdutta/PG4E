# Notes on Cryptography, Hashing and NIST (Talk with Bruce Schneier)

---

Cryptography doesn't actually protect you. It just makes it harder for the bad guys to get your data.
So if NSA, CIA, FBI, etc. want to get your data, they will get it. But if you use good cryptography, it will be harder for them to get it. So if you are not in their priority list, you are less likely to be targeted and they will have to spend more resources to get your data. So it is a good idea to use good cryptography to protect your data.

Without cryptography, we would have no privacy and government can have access to all our data ,and they will store the data.
Cryptography forces organizations to go after criminals, not everyone. It is a way to protect the privacy of the general public while still allowing law enforcement to do their job.

Cryptography doesn't actually provide any security, because cryptography is just mathematics. When we trust the cryptography, it means we trust the math. But math doesn't have agency. You have to code the math correctly in a program and use that program correctly.

When NSA or other organization break cryptography, they don't break the math, they break something else. They might find a flaw in the implementation, or they might find a way to get around the cryptography, or they might find a way to get the key, or they might find a way to get the password, or they might find a way to get the data before it is encrypted, or they might find a way to get the data after it is decrypted.

Complexity is worst enemy of security. More complex you make your system less secure it is going to be.

NIST try to standardize different mathematical operation and implementation behavior to reduce some mistakes.
Security needs as few options as possible.

Security essentially is adversarial.
